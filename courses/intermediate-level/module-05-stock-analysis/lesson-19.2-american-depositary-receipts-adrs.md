## American Depositary Receipts Fundamentals

### What are ADRs?
American Depositary Receipts (ADRs) are negotiable certificates issued by US banks that represent ownership in shares of foreign companies. They allow investors to buy foreign stocks through domestic exchanges without dealing with foreign exchange directly.

### ADR Structure
- **Issued by**: US banks (JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, etc.)
- **Underlying**: Shares of foreign company held in custody
- **Trading**: Trade on US stock exchanges
- **Currency**: Denominated in US dollars
- **Rights**: Provide same economic rights as underlying shares

### How ADRs Work
- **Custody Bank**: Foreign company shares held by US custodian bank
- **ADR Issuance**: US bank issues ADRs representing fractional ownership
- **Trading**: ADRs trade on US exchanges like US stocks
- **Dividends**: Dividends paid in USD after currency conversion
- **Corporate Actions**: ADR holders participate in corporate actions

## Types of ADRs

### By Level of Information

#### Level I ADRs
- **Trading**: Trade on over-the-counter (OTC) markets
- **Reporting**: Minimal reporting requirements
- **Eligibility**: Easier for foreign companies
- **Liquidity**: Lower liquidity than listed ADRs
- **Example**: Many European and Asian companies

#### Level II ADRs
- **Listing**: Listed on major US exchanges (NYSE, NASDAQ)
- **Reporting**: Full SEC reporting requirements
- **Compliance**: Must comply with US GAAP
- **Liquidity**: Higher liquidity and visibility
- **Example**: Large international companies

#### Level III ADRs
- **Listing**: Listed on major US exchanges
- **Issuance**: Can raise capital in US markets
- **Requirements**: Most stringent requirements
- **Liquidity**: Highest liquidity and access
- **Example**: Major international companies raising US capital

### By Sponsorship

#### Sponsored ADRs
- **Company Involvement**: Foreign company actively involved
- **Corporate Actions**: Company directly handles corporate actions
- **Reporting**: Company provides financial reporting
- **Cost**: Higher costs but better service
- **Quality**: Higher quality and more reliable

#### Unsponsored ADRs
- **Multiple Banks**: Multiple banks may issue same ADR
- **Limited Involvement**: Limited foreign company involvement
- **Corporate Actions**: Banks handle corporate actions
- **Cost**: Lower costs
- **Quality**: Lower quality and potential confusion

## ADR Investment Process

### Opening International Trading Account

#### Requirements
- **KYC**: Complete KYC with international broker
- **Documentation**: Passport, address proof, bank details
- **FEMA**: FEMA approval for international investments
- **LRS**: Liberalized Remittance Scheme compliance
- **Broker**: Choose broker offering international trading

#### Account Types
- **Regular**: Regular trading account for ADRs
- **Portfolio**: Portfolio investment scheme
- **PINS**: Portfolio Investment Number scheme
- **Requirements**: Different requirements for each
- **Limits**: LRS limits apply ($250,000 annually)

### ADR Trading Process

#### Order Placement
- **Broker Platform**: Place orders through broker platform
- **Symbol**: Use ADR symbol (AAPL, MSFT, etc.)
- **Quantity**: Specify number of ADRs
- **Price**: Market or limit orders
- **Currency**: USD-denominated transactions

#### Settlement Process
- **T+2**: Trade date + 2 business days settlement
- **USD**: Settlement in US dollars
- **Broker**: Local broker handles conversion
- **Custody**: Custody in Demat account
- **Corporate**: Corporate actions handled automatically
